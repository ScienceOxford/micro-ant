Choose Materials and Designing your Body

These instructions discuss the properties of some of the materials you could use for your robot's body, and describes how to create your design by hand or on a computer.

Materials

Cardboard

  • Easy to cut by hand
  • Easy to cut by Cricut
  • Can be scored to create creases and joints, so the whole body can be created from one piece
  • Lightweight
  • Not very strong
  • Easy to bend into shapes
  • Opaque

High Impact Polystyrene (HIPS)

  • Easy to cut by hand
  • Can be cut by Cricut
  • Can be scored to create creases and joints, but this weakens it
  • Lightweight
  • Strong
  • Can be bent into shapes
  • Opaque

Medium Density Fibreboard (MDF)

  • Cannot be cut by hand
  • Easy to cut by laser cutter
  • Cannot be scored
  • Heavy
  • Strong
  • Cannot be bent into shapes
  • Opaque

Plastic bottles

  • Easy to cut by hand
  • Cannot be cut by Cricut or laser cutter
  • Can be scored to create creases and joints
  • Lightweight
  • Strong
  • Can be bent into shapes
  • Transparent

Corrigated plastic

  • Easy to cut by hand
  • Can be cut by laser cutter
  • Can be scored to create creases and joints, but this weakens it
  • Lightweight
  • Strong
  • Cannot be bent into shapes
  • Opaque

Design

Step 1 measure your robot

Measure your robot's length, width and height, and write the measurements down.

This is the minimum size you will need to design your body.


Step 2 understanding nets

A good way to design your first prototype is to design a net.

A net is a flat (2D) shape which you can fold up to make a 3D shape.

You can see an animation of a net turning into a 3D shape on the Maths is Fun website.

If you are in a workshop with us, have a go with the Polydron shapes to see what kinds of designs you can make!


Step 3 creating your prototype

To create a box that fits your robot, you need to think about all the sides, and connect them togther as a net.

You do not want to enclose your robot inside a cube, as then the wheels won't be able to touch the ground!

So to start with, try making a box with two open sides that your robot fits inside.

  • The top piece will be as wide and as long as your robot.
  • The bottom piece is the same as the top piece.
  • The back piece will be as wide and as high as your robot.
  • The front piece will be the same as the back piece.

The one measurement all of these pieces have in common is the width of your robot, so you can connect them all together to make a net.

Create a net using your own robot's measurements on a piece of card.

Cut around the outside, and score the inside lines to create a bend.

  • Does it fit your robot?
  • What adjustments do you need to make to allow your robot to drive freely?
  • Do you need to make any cut-outs, such as to reach the charging port?
  • How could you change the design to make a wedge shape?

Iterate your design until you are happy with it.

If you are cutting the body by hand...

Get a piece of your chosen material large enough for your design.

Check whether your material will allow you to score lines or not!

If it does, cut out the outside of your design, and score the inside lines. Then assemble around your robot.

If it does not, cut the outside and the inside lines, so all of the pieces are seperate. Then use tape to assemble around your robot.

If you are cutting the body on the laser cutter...

Get a piece of your chosen material large enough for your design.

Use Tinkercad to recreate your design - paying attention to the measurements.

If this is your first time designing for a laser cutter, have a look at our instructions first!

When you have finished, you will have an .svg or .dxf file, which you can bring to us for laser cutting.

If you are cutting the body on the cricut...

Get a piece of your chosen material large enough for your design.

Use Design Space to recreate your design from shapes - paying attention to the measurements.

If this is your first time creating your own design on Design Space, talk to a workshop leader or check out the Cricut tutorials first!

If you want to use score lines to fold your net, instead of cutting each piece seperately, you will need to add score lines to your design.

After placing your score lines, click on all the pieces of your net except the score lines and weld them together.

This will merge your shapes, making sure that the Cricut machine does not cut the inside lines.

When you have finished, you will have a Design Space project, which you can bring to us to get cut on the Cricut.
